# Project 1: Ear Shield

## Hero / Project Overview

**Title**
Ear Shield

**Subtitle**
Smart whale monitoring and echo enhancement

**One-line summary**
Ear Shield explores how acoustic monitoring, bionic device design, and an interactive public installation can make the impact of human underwater noise on toothed whales visible and understandable.

**Project type**
Speculative product system / environmental design / interactive installation

**Role**
Concept designer, researcher, prototyper, and interactive installation designer. Confirm whether this was individual or group work.

**Duration**
To confirm.

**Tools**
Python, Fourier analysis, Arduino, ultrasonic sensor, TouchDesigner, Blender, physical model making.

**Methods**
Marine noise research, species research, sound-wave analysis, bionic ideation, technical prototyping, gesture interaction, stakeholder mapping, exhibition communication.

**Output**
A speculative whale-mounted acoustic monitoring device concept, a whale health monitoring logic, and an interactive installation demonstrating how noise interference affects sperm whale perception.

## Context

Whale strandings are often discussed as isolated ecological events, but research into toothed whales suggests that underwater noise can disrupt the acoustic systems they rely on to survive. Sperm whales and other toothed whales use echolocation for orientation, hunting, and communication. When human-made noise and sonar interfere with these signals, whales may lose spatial judgment, experience stress, deviate from migration routes, or face increased stranding risk.

The project began from this tension: whales live in an environment where sound is a primary way of sensing the world, while human activity increasingly changes that acoustic environment.

## Challenge

The challenge was to make an invisible environmental problem legible. Underwater noise cannot be easily seen by the public, and the relationship between acoustic interference, whale health, and stranding is complex.

The project asked:

- How can sound-based whale perception be translated into a visual and interactive experience?
- How might a monitoring device support whale health research without ignoring the biological and environmental context?
- How can a public installation help people understand the consequences of human noise beneath the ocean surface?

## My Role

The portfolio shows work across research, technical experimentation, device ideation, and exhibition prototyping. I researched toothed whale biology, echolocation, human noise interference, and sperm whale sensing. I developed a bionic device concept inspired by octopus suction structures, explored how acoustic data could be analyzed through Fourier-based methods, and built an interactive installation using Arduino, TouchDesigner, and Blender to simulate how human noise can disrupt whale perception.

Confirm exact wording once team structure is known.

## Process

### 1. Understanding whale perception through sound

The research focused on toothed whales because they rely on high-frequency echolocation. The portfolio compares toothed and baleen whales, then narrows into sperm whales as a key subject because of their deep-diving behavior, limited vision underwater, and reliance on acoustic sensing.

This established an important design constraint: the project should not treat whales as visual animals. It needed to communicate a sound-first way of navigating the world.

### 2. Mapping the causes and effects of noise interference

The background research connects human noise, seabed activity, military sonar, and whale stranding events. The PDF highlights human noise as a major factor in disrupting whale communication and orientation.

This research shaped the central narrative of the project: the issue is not only that whales are harmed, but that the sensory system they depend on is being interrupted.

### 3. Exploring a bionic monitoring device

The device concept was inspired by the suction ability of octopus claws. This led to a spiral adsorption structure intended to attach to a whale without relying on invasive fastening.

The device was designed as a temporary monitoring system. It would attach near the whale's nasal area, capture sound signals, support health analysis, adapt to deep water movement, and detach for recovery.

### 4. Testing a sound-analysis logic

The technical exploration used Python libraries including pyaudio, numpy, librosa, matplotlib, os, and csv to explore whale sound processing. The PDF compares healthy and abnormal sound data through MFCC, Chroma, and Mel spectrogram analysis.

This part of the project demonstrates a data-informed prototyping mindset. The aim was not to claim a finished medical diagnostic system, but to test how acoustic patterns might be translated into a monitoring framework.

### 5. Translating the system into a public interaction

The installation translated echolocation and noise interference into an experience that audiences could physically affect. When the audience moved closer, ultrasonic sensors changed the behavior of a whale model, causing it to disperse into particles. The gesture interaction and screen-based whale environment helped visitors sense how human presence and noise could distort underwater perception.

## Key Insights

**Insight 1: For toothed whales, noise is not background. It interferes with orientation.**

The project reframes human-made noise as a direct interruption of perception rather than a distant environmental side effect.

**Insight 2: A conservation issue becomes more understandable when people can experience the sensory loss.**

The installation lets audiences feel the relationship between human interference and whale disorientation through interaction, rather than only reading an explanation.

**Insight 3: Technical speculation is stronger when it is connected to public education.**

The whale-mounted device concept and the exhibition prototype work together: one imagines a monitoring system, while the other builds public awareness of the problem the system responds to.

## Design Response

Ear Shield proposes a dual response: a speculative acoustic monitoring device for toothed whales, and a public interactive installation that communicates the impact of human noise.

The device contains a data processing center, underwater camera, intelligent adsorption system, and spiral suction structure. It is positioned as a tool for monitoring whale health, tracking acoustic signals, and supporting research into underwater noise.

The installation uses gesture interaction, ultrasonic sensing, and a particle-based whale simulation to show how noise disrupts whale perception. It turns the whale's acoustic world into a visual, embodied experience for the public.

## Outcome / Impact

The project was developed into an exhibition installation with physical model elements and public interaction. The PDF documents visitors engaging with the work and includes a stakeholder map for future collaboration with research institutions, public education organizations, government bodies, environmental groups, and technology suppliers.

Do not claim implementation in marine research unless confirmed.

## Reflection

This project demonstrates my ability to connect environmental research, speculative systems thinking, technical prototyping, and public communication. It shows how I use design not only to propose an object, but to create a bridge between scientific complexity and public understanding.

The strongest part of the project is the translation from invisible underwater sound to tangible interaction. For the next iteration, I would refine the scientific validation of the acoustic analysis model, define ethical constraints for whale-mounted devices, and develop the installation into a clearer educational experience for museums or marine conservation contexts.

# Project 2: SeedWalkers

## Hero / Project Overview

**Title**
SeedWalkers

**Subtitle**
An AR service system for reconnecting fragmented urban biodiversity

**One-line summary**
SeedWalkers turns urban planting into a participatory service experience, using seed cards, AR interaction, and city-scale ecological data to encourage plant diversity and rebuild fragmented urban ecosystems.

**Project type**
Service design / environmental design / AR user experience

**Role**
Service designer, environmental researcher, system mapper, and AR experience designer. Confirm individual or team context.

**Duration**
March to July 2024.

**Tools**
ENVI, ArcGIS, NDVI analysis, Blender, Unity, low-fidelity UI prototyping.

**Methods**
Climate and carbon research, biodiversity research, urban vegetation analysis, stakeholder mapping, service blueprinting, user flow design, AR prototyping.

**Output**
A city-scale ecological service concept combining seed storage devices, seed cards, an AR planting app, user rankings, and biodiversity restoration feedback.

## Context

Many cities invest heavily in green space, but urban vegetation can still become ecologically weak when planting is overly aesthetic, homogeneous, and fragmented. In Changsha, the project identifies a gap between the city's carbon emissions and the carbon absorption capacity of its vegetation.

The project links this urban carbon challenge to biodiversity. A richer mix of trees, shrubs, and ground cover can provide more habitats, support more species, and strengthen the carbon cycle of the city.

## Challenge

The challenge was not simply to design another green app. The project needed to connect multiple scales:

- city-level carbon neutrality and vegetation data
- biodiversity and plant diversity
- public participation
- seed dispersal and ecological restoration
- an experience that could motivate users to take part

The design question became: how can people help reconnect a fragmented urban ecosystem through a service experience that is both educational and participatory?

## My Role

The PDF shows a full service-design process, from environmental research and remote-sensing analysis to app logic, stakeholder mapping, and AR prototyping. I explored Changsha's carbon and vegetation conditions, mapped the relationship between plant diversity and biodiversity, designed the user journey around seed cards and AR planting, and prototyped the supporting visual and interaction system.

Confirm exact team contribution before publishing.

## Process

### 1. Framing the ecological problem

The project starts from carbon neutrality and urban greenhouse gas emissions. It uses Changsha as the research site and compares current carbon absorption by vegetation against emissions. The PDF states that a significant portion of carbon emissions remains unabsorbed by existing vegetation.

This framed the project around ecological function, not only landscape appearance.

### 2. Connecting plant diversity to biodiversity

The research maps how tree diversity supports habitats, food, shelter, nitrogen availability, and more stable ecosystems. It also studies local flora and fauna in Changsha, including birds that can carry and disperse seeds.

This insight shifted the project from "plant more greenery" to "increase diversity and reconnect ecological relationships."

### 3. Using spatial and vegetation analysis

ENVI was used to extract vegetation and classify land-use types from remote-sensing imagery. ArcGIS and NDVI analysis supported the calculation of natural vegetation, artificial green space, and carbon-related values.

This technical layer gave the service concept a stronger environmental basis and helped identify where ecological intervention could happen.

### 4. Designing the service framework

The service combines a seed storage device, a mobile app, seed cards, AR planting, ecological feedback, rankings, and city progress tracking.

Users scan a QR code on a seed storage device, receive a seed card, are guided to a planting location, view the seed model in AR, and receive feedback such as badges, carbon sequestration values, and biodiversity progress.

### 5. Mapping stakeholders and backstage operations

The service blueprint includes users, technical staff, seed card manufacturers, garden designers, government departments, urban planning bureaus, garden bureaus, and maintenance teams.

This shows the project as a service ecosystem rather than a standalone interface.

## Key Insights

**Insight 1: Urban greening can look successful while still being ecologically fragile.**

A city can have green coverage but still suffer from monoculture, fragmentation, and insufficient ecological diversity.

**Insight 2: Biodiversity needs participation and maintenance, not only planning.**

The project positions residents as active connectors in an ecological network, supported by government, technical systems, and landscape maintenance.

**Insight 3: AR can make ecological restoration feel immediate.**

Because plant growth and ecosystem recovery are slow, AR gives users a visible preview of contribution, helping them understand how a seed action connects to a larger system.

## Design Response

SeedWalkers proposes a participatory ecological service. Users receive seed cards from public storage devices and use the app to place, view, and track planting actions through AR. The service turns urban biodiversity restoration into a distributed journey: receive a seed, find a location, plant, view growth, track contribution, and connect with other participants.

The system also supports city-level monitoring. Government and maintenance teams can supervise deployment, manage planting areas, and coordinate ecological restoration through the service blueprint.

## Outcome / Impact

The project produced a complete service concept, low-fidelity app flow, stakeholder map, service blueprint, AR interaction logic, and 3D seed-card prototype. The PDF shows technical experimentation across ENVI, ArcGIS, Blender, and Unity.

No real-world pilot metrics are shown in the PDF, so the outcome should be framed as a concept and prototype, not a launched service.

## Reflection

SeedWalkers demonstrates my ability to design across environmental systems, public participation, and digital interaction. The project combines ecological research with service design structure, showing how spatial data and biodiversity logic can become a more accessible user experience.

The next iteration should focus on validating the service with city stakeholders, testing whether users understand the ecological feedback, and defining the maintenance model for planted areas.

# Project 3: Machine Mapping

## Hero / Project Overview

**Title**
Machine Mapping

**Subtitle**
A reflective experiment on machine learning and social cognition

**One-line summary**
Machine Mapping uses a machine-learning metaphor, participatory discussion, and data visualization to question how color stereotypes are formed, repeated, and absorbed by both humans and machines.

**Project type**
Interactive experiment / critical design / creative technology

**Role**
Researcher, interaction designer, creative technologist, and visualization designer. Confirm individual or team context.

**Duration**
To confirm.

**Tools**
Arduino, Python, Vosk, image search, Blender, Blender Python scripts, shader nodes.

**Methods**
Cultural research, participatory discussion, keyword extraction, speech recognition, image dataset collection, color-block extraction, 3D visualization.

**Output**
An interactive machine-brain installation that listens to group discussions, extracts keywords, collects related images, and visualizes how social meaning becomes machine memory.

## Context

Colors often feel natural or self-evident, but their meanings are shaped by culture, social agreement, markets, media, and personal experience. Pink can be associated with femininity, blue with trust or professionalism, and green with environmental responsibility, even though these associations are not fixed truths.

Machine Mapping begins from a simple question shown in the portfolio: who gave color its meaning?

## Challenge

The project needed to avoid giving a single moral conclusion. Instead, it aimed to create a reflective situation where participants could see how their own conversations and cultural assumptions shape a machine's understanding.

The challenge was to design an open-ended system that could:

- collect human discussion as input
- translate speech into keywords
- connect keywords to image search results
- visualize the machine's changing memory
- reveal social stereotypes without simply judging participants

## My Role

The portfolio shows research into the cultural, commercial, and social construction of color meaning, followed by an interactive system using Arduino, Python, Vosk speech recognition, image search, and Blender visualization. I designed the process by which group discussion becomes machine memory and used the installation to make social cognition visible.

Confirm exact team context and whether all technical implementation was built individually.

## Process

### 1. Researching color as a social construction

The project examines color meaning through several lenses: social construction, cultural change, business and markets, and historical shifts. The PDF traces examples of pink's changing meaning across time and explores how industries and social groups attach meaning to color.

This research established that color stereotypes are learned and repeated, not neutral.

### 2. Designing a machine-learning metaphor

Instead of building a conventional classifier, the project uses machine learning as a metaphor for social cognition. Participants discuss colors from cultural, commercial, and social perspectives. Their words become the machine's input.

The more people speak, the richer and more biased the machine's memory becomes.

### 3. Turning speech into searchable data

The technical system records participant speech, identifies keywords, and searches for related images. Python and Vosk are used for real-time audio and speech recognition. The system breaks down Chinese sentences and phrases, then uses the extracted keywords as the basis for image collection.

### 4. Extracting and synthesizing visual memory

The searched images are processed into color blocks and stored as part of the machine's memory bank. The PDF shows how repeated image patterns can produce visual feedback, such as pink becoming associated with women through the frequency and pattern of collected data.

### 5. Visualizing the machine brain

Blender is used to create a machine-brain space. The portfolio describes image storage locations, a brain-like repository, and shader-node logic for dynamic visualization.

This makes the machine's learning process spatial and visible, rather than hidden inside a black-box interface.

## Key Insights

**Insight 1: A machine can reproduce social assumptions because its input is already social.**

The project shows that bias does not begin inside the machine. It can enter through language, culture, search results, and human discussion.

**Insight 2: Open-ended feedback can be more powerful than a fixed conclusion.**

Instead of telling participants what to think, the installation reflects their associations back to them and asks them to interpret the result.

**Insight 3: Visualization can make abstract cognition discussable.**

By turning keywords, image searches, and color associations into a visible brain space, the project helps participants talk about how meaning is formed.

## Design Response

Machine Mapping creates a participatory loop between people and machine. Participants discuss a color, the system listens and extracts keywords, related images are collected, color information is processed, and the machine produces visual feedback based on its accumulated memory.

The installation positions the machine as a mirror of social cognition. It does not present machine intelligence as neutral or objective. Instead, it asks viewers to consider how social data, repeated associations, and cultural history shape what a machine appears to know.

## Outcome / Impact

The project resulted in a working interactive experiment and documented video. The PDF shows a four-step discussion structure using blue, green, and pink, followed by a final moment where participants ask the machine what the colors mean.

The available evidence supports framing this as a reflective prototype and critical design experiment. Do not claim formal research impact unless additional testing exists.

## Reflection

Machine Mapping demonstrates my interest in AI and machine learning as cultural systems, not only technical tools. It shows how I use physical computing, Python, and 3D visualization to build experiences that help people question hidden assumptions.

For a future iteration, I would make the dataset and search logic more transparent, compare different cultural groups, and design a clearer post-interaction reflection format so participants can discuss what changed in their thinking.

# Project 4: Cross-Culture Icebreaker

## Hero / Project Overview

**Title**
Cross-Culture Icebreaker

**Subtitle**
A social kit for Chinese international students

**One-line summary**
Cross-Culture Icebreaker is a group project that uses a social pack and board-game experience to help Chinese international students reduce social pressure, exchange cultural backgrounds, and build early connections in unfamiliar university environments.

**Project type**
Service design / social design / game-based experience design

**Role**
Group project. Confirm your specific role before publishing. Visible contributions include research, questionnaire analysis, personas, service system mapping, user flow design, and prototype development.

**Duration**
To confirm.

**Tools**
Questionnaire survey, interviews, personas, 5W1H, service system map, user flow, physical prototyping, business canvas.

**Methods**
Social research, international student research, cultural comparison, co-experience design, game mechanics, stakeholder mapping, prototype testing.

**Output**
A social package with Polaroid camera, film, journal, and tools, plus a board-game kit designed to create structured, low-pressure cross-cultural interaction.

## Context

Chinese international students often arrive at overseas universities with strong academic preparation but face social barriers in unfamiliar environments. The PDF identifies social anxiety, cultural conflict, loneliness, language barriers, fear of rejection, and lack of social opportunities as important problems.

The project focuses on the beginning of university life, when students are adapting to new social norms and may need structured opportunities to connect with others.

## Challenge

The challenge was to design an experience that could break the ice without forcing uncomfortable direct interaction.

The project needed to support students who may:

- worry about rejection or misunderstanding
- struggle with language barriers
- lack social opportunities
- stay mainly within familiar Chinese-speaking circles
- want friendship but feel unsure how to start

The design question became: how can a social experience create enough structure and playfulness for students to interact naturally?

## My Role

The portfolio identifies this as a group project. The visible work includes research into Chinese international students, a questionnaire with 117 respondents, personas, 5W1H framing, pain-point mapping, service system mapping, user flows, a social package concept, a board-game kit, and business model thinking.

Before publishing, define your exact contribution in the group: research lead, concept lead, service mapping, prototyping, visual design, or presentation.

## Process

### 1. Understanding the student experience

The project begins with data on Chinese students studying abroad and the social challenges they face. The PDF highlights problems such as cultural conflict, loneliness, employment pressure, language barriers, and social anxiety.

This helped frame the issue as more than shyness. It is a social adaptation problem shaped by education background, language confidence, culture, and access to opportunities.

### 2. Comparing educational and social expectations

The research compares Chinese and US education contexts. It suggests that some students may have had fewer opportunities to build communication, teamwork, and public social skills before studying abroad.

This comparison helped identify why open-ended social events can feel intimidating and why a more structured interaction format might help.

### 3. Using survey data and personas

A questionnaire with 117 Chinese students identified major causes of social problems, including cultural adaptation, fear of rejection, language barriers, and lack of social opportunities.

Two personas, Susan and Zheng Ran, represent different student needs but share common barriers: desire for connection, fear of misunderstanding, language difficulty, and lack of suitable opportunities.

### 4. Designing for indirect interaction

The concept explores how students might socialize without beginning with intense face-to-face interaction. The team considered nonverbal communication, hobbies, festivals, customs, notebooks, Polaroids, board games, and reward mechanisms.

This led to two connected outputs: a social pack and a board-game kit.

### 5. Creating the social pack

The social pack includes a Polaroid camera, film, journal, and tools. It moves between students over several days. Each participant documents part of their life, adds to the journal, and passes the kit to the next person.

This creates a slow, indirect form of cultural exchange before direct group interaction.

### 6. Creating the game kit

The game kit brings nine participants together. The board includes 36 tiles divided into nine areas. Players solve challenges, rotate blocks, connect routes, unlock notes, and discuss the experiences behind them.

The challenge types include psychological, regional, language, and cultural adaptation themes. The game uses shared problem-solving to reduce pressure and encourage conversation.

## Key Insights

**Insight 1: Students may need structure before spontaneity.**

For socially anxious students, an unstructured event can increase pressure. A game or shared object gives people a reason to interact.

**Insight 2: Indirect exchange can make cultural sharing feel safer.**

The Polaroid and journal format lets students express daily life and identity without beginning with direct verbal performance.

**Insight 3: Social design needs stakeholders, not only participants.**

The service system includes schools, organizers, communities, sponsors, suppliers, and students. This makes the concept more realistic as a hosted activity.

## Design Response

Cross-Culture Icebreaker proposes a two-part social system.

The social pack creates a chain of personal documentation and exchange. Students use the camera and journal to share their daily routines, cultural background, and interests, then pass the kit to another participant.

The board-game kit creates a shared event where participants solve puzzles and unlock notes together. The game mechanics encourage cooperation, discussion, and cultural storytelling while reducing the pressure of direct self-introduction.

Together, the two formats move students from indirect exchange to group interaction.

## Outcome / Impact

The project produced a physical prototype, service system map, user flows, business canvas, social package, board game, and demo process. The PDF includes participant quotes describing curiosity, nervousness, and engagement when receiving and delivering the social package.

The evidence supports framing the project as a tested concept or prototype demonstration. Do not claim long-term improvement in social integration unless more follow-up data exists.

## Reflection

This project demonstrates my ability to work with social research, service design, and game-based interaction. It shows how design can lower the emotional threshold for participation by using objects, rules, and shared tasks.

For the next iteration, I would clarify the facilitator role, test the kit with a larger group of students, measure whether participants continue contact after the activity, and simplify the game rules for faster onboarding.
